Eating Bugs

Small Animals – Big Change

Entomophagy

The Practice of Consuming Edible Insects

Mealworms, crickets, termites: about 1900 insect species are consumed in 113 countries around the world, primarily in Africa, Asia, and Latin America. According to the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations, about 2 billion people have insects as part of their traditional diet.

Superheroes

How Insects Can Change the World

Switching from a meat- to an insect-based diet would have transformative effects: up to 30% of the world's land surface could be reclaimed from the livestock industry, and we could eliminate up to 18% of our global greenhouse gas emissions. Average food prices in most countries could see a drop of up to 33%.

Sustainable Bugs

How Does the Farming of Insects Impact our Planet?

Eating animals takes a toll on our environment. Consuming bugs however is the much more sustainable choice than traditional livestock. Crickets for example need six times less feed than cattle, and half as much as pigs and broiler chickens to produce the same amount of protein. They also produce significantly less greenhouse gases and ammonia, and can be fed on organic waste streams.

Nutritious Bugs

Do They Have Enough Protein?

Edible Insects are very nutritious and a healthy alternative to traditional staples like chicken, beef, or pork. They are not only high in good quality protein, but also vitamins, amino acids, calcium, and iron. Insect-eating advocates like the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations say that cultivating edible insects could stave-off world hunger and future food insecurity.

Bugs as a Snack

Why not Try a Spicy Superworm?

Mexico-born Monica Martinez (40) and her company Don Bugito (founded 2011) produce insect snacks with Prehispanic flavors. From her kitchen in "La Cocina" in San Francisco's Mission district she cooks up Spicy Superworms, Chocolate Covered Crickets, and Toffee Mealworms – all farmed in California. With her mobile food truck and online shop, she spreads a message of food sustainabilty. Why not switch to one insect-based meal a week?
